Dollar‑cost averaging vs lump sum: which is better?

Lump sum invests earlier (more time in the market). Dollar-cost averaging spreads timing risk. Use the calculator to model both.

What the research actually says

Studies consistently show that lump sum investing beats dollar-cost averaging (DCA) roughly two-thirds of the time over a 12-month deployment period. The reason is simple: markets go up more often than they go down, so money invested immediately tends to outperform money held in cash waiting to be deployed. Vanguard's research on US, UK and Australian markets found lump sum investing outperformed DCA by an average of 2-3% over 12 months. However, "on average" hides a lot — in the one-third of cases where markets fell, DCA came out ahead.

Lump sum investing

How it works: you invest the full amount immediately, then it grows from day one.

  • Pro: maximum time in the market — every day invested is a day compounding.
  • Pro: statistically outperforms DCA in most market environments.
  • Con: psychologically hard — investing right before a 20% drop feels terrible.
  • Con: if you don't have a lump sum, this isn't an option anyway.

Best for: windfalls, inheritance, proceeds from selling property, or anyone comfortable with short-term volatility.

Dollar-cost averaging (DCA)

How it works: you split the amount into equal portions and invest at regular intervals (weekly, monthly).

  • Pro: reduces timing risk — you buy more units when prices are low, fewer when high.
  • Pro: psychologically easier — a 20% drop after your first instalment is less painful.
  • Pro: matches how most people actually earn money (salary paid monthly).
  • Con: cash waiting to be deployed earns lower returns than invested money.
  • Con: statistically underperforms lump sum in rising markets.

Best for: regular salary investors, people with high loss aversion, or anyone deploying a large sum who would panic-sell after a drop.

The Australian context

For most Australians, DCA is simply how investing works in practice — you invest from your salary each month into ETFs like VAS or VGS. The lump sum vs DCA question mainly arises when you have a windfall (inheritance, property sale, redundancy payout). In that case, a short DCA period of 3-6 months is a reasonable middle ground — you capture most of the lump sum advantage while reducing the emotional risk of a bad entry point. Superannuation contributions are by nature DCA — your employer contributes monthly, smoothing your entry price automatically over decades.

How to model both in the calculator

  • Lump sum: enter the full amount as your initial investment, set monthly contribution to $0, and run for your timeline.
  • DCA: set initial investment to $0, enter a monthly contribution equal to your lump sum divided by your deployment period (e.g. $60,000 ÷ 6 months = $10,000/month), run for your timeline.
  • Compare: the lump sum result will usually be higher, but the DCA path will feel smoother.

FAQ

Is lump sum always better?

Often it wins mathematically because money is invested sooner, but timing risk and behaviour matter.

Is DCA safer?

It can feel safer because you spread entry points over time, but it can reduce returns if markets rise.

What if I’m anxious about investing a lump sum?

A reasonable compromise is a short DCA period (e.g. 3–6 months), then commit to the plan.

Can I model a hybrid plan?

Yes. Use a smaller lump sum plus monthly contributions.

Does the calculator predict markets?

No. It uses an average return assumption for education and scenario testing.
