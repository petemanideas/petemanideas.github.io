What retiring at 70 really means financially

Retiring at 70 gives you the longest possible runway to build wealth, which dramatically reduces the monthly investment required. Starting from age 35 with 35 years to invest, reaching a $1,000,000 portfolio only requires around $555 per month at 7% returns — compared to $1,920 per month for a 55 retirement. While the portfolio size needed is the same, the extra time means compounding does far more of the heavy lifting. In Australia, retiring at 70 also means full access to superannuation and potentially the Age Pension, making your investment portfolio go even further. Retiring at 70 in Australia: the advantages

Retiring at 70 in Australia comes with significant financial advantages. You've already had full super access for 10 years, you're past the Age Pension eligibility age (currently 67), and you've had an extra decade of compounding on your investments. The Age Pension (around $28,000 for singles, $42,000 for couples annually) substantially reduces how much your portfolio needs to generate — so even a $500,000 portfolio can support a comfortable retirement when combined with pension entitlements. Super in pension phase remains tax-free, and the minimum drawdown rates (starting at 5% at age 70) ensure you're drawing enough to benefit from the tax-free environment. For many Australians, retiring at 70 is less about "having enough" and more about choosing to keep working — the numbers often already work comfortably by this age.

FAQ

Should I use 3% or 4%?

Use both. 3% is conservative. 4% is a common guideline. Compare the range.

Does this include pensions/super?

Not automatically. If you expect guaranteed income later, subtract it from what your portfolio must provide.

Is this a guarantee?

No — it’s a scenario model to help you plan. Real returns vary.

What if my target looks too high?

Lower spending, delay retirement, increase contributions, or plan a partial retirement approach.

What’s the best next step?

Choose a conservative plan you can stick with, then increase contributions gradually.
